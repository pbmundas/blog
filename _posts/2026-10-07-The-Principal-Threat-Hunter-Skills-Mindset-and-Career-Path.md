Two hunters, both with eight years of experience, both technically sharp. One's still called "senior." One just got promoted to "principal." The difference usually isn't technical skill in the narrow sense both can write a solid query, both understand attacker TTPs deeply. The difference is what they do when nobody's told them what to hunt for, and that's a much harder thing to teach or even fully articulate.



## Execution Versus Direction Is the Real Divide
A senior hunter, even a very good one, is typically excellent at executing a well-scoped investigation given a hypothesis, they'll chase it down thoroughly, correctly, and efficiently. A principal hunter is the one generating the hypotheses in the first place, often for problems the organization hasn't fully articulated yet. They're not waiting for a threat intel report to tell them what to look for; they're synthesizing signals from multiple sources recent incidents in the industry, subtle shifts in the environment, gaps they've noticed nobody's addressed into a genuinely new line of investigation nobody had thought to pursue.



This shows up concretely in how each role responds to ambiguity. Hand a senior hunter a vague, half-formed concern "something about our vendor access feels off, not sure why" and a strong one will ask good clarifying questions and start investigating once scoped. A principal hunter is often the one raising that vague concern in the first place, having noticed something nobody explicitly asked them to look for, and then doing the work of turning that instinct into a structured, testable hypothesis themselves.



## Cross-Domain Fluency, Not Just Depth
Senior hunters are often deep specialists genuinely excellent at, say, Windows endpoint forensics, or network traffic analysis, with real mastery in that lane. Principal hunters tend to have built enough fluency across multiple domains endpoint, network, cloud, identity, sometimes OT to recognize when an investigation that started in one domain actually needs expertise from another, and to at least start that cross-domain work themselves before looping in a specialist.



This isn't about being equally expert in everything that's not realistic or even necessary. It's about pattern recognition broad enough to know that a credential access finding might have an interesting cloud IAM angle worth pulling, even if cloud isn't their primary specialty, rather than staying narrowly within their comfort zone and missing the connection entirely.



## Influence Without Direct Authority
A structural reality of principal-level roles across most technical disciplines, hunting included: the title usually comes without direct management authority over other hunters, but with a real expectation of shaping how the whole team operates hunt methodology, hypothesis prioritization frameworks, mentoring junior and senior hunters on investigative technique.



This requires a genuinely different skill than technical excellence alone: the ability to influence and teach without formal authority. A principal hunter who can't communicate why a particular hunt hypothesis matters, or can't mentor a promising junior hunter through developing their own investigative instincts, isn't fully operating at that level regardless of how sharp their individual technical work is. I've seen technically brilliant hunters plateau at senior specifically because this communication and mentoring dimension never developed alongside the technical depth.



## Comfort With Genuine Ambiguity and Being Wrong
Principal-level hunt work often means pursuing hypotheses that don't pan out, publicly, more often than junior work does because the hypotheses are more speculative and less validated by existing playbooks or prior precedent. That requires a real comfort with being wrong in front of the team, documenting a dead end honestly, and moving to the next hypothesis without treating a null result as a personal failure.



This is a mindset shift as much as a skill, and it's one of the harder things to develop deliberately. Hunters who need every investigation to produce a clean, validated finding to feel successful tend to gravitate toward safer, more certain hypotheses which is a reasonable instinct early in a career, but it caps the ceiling of what they'll ever discover. The most valuable principal-level findings I've seen came from hunters willing to chase a hunch that felt genuinely uncertain, document three dead ends along the way, and stay with it anyway until the fourth angle actually produced something real.



## Building Toward This Deliberately
The path from senior to principal isn't primarily about accumulating more years or more certifications plenty of hunters stall at senior for a decade without ever developing the hypothesis-generation and cross-domain fluency that actually defines the next level. It's built through deliberate exposure: taking on ambiguous, under-scoped problems on purpose rather than always working from a clear brief, actively cross-training into adjacent domains, and practicing the mentoring and communication skills that principal-level influence actually depends on.
